Water

Water can be found everywhere. From the vast salty oceans to the dry desert heat water is the life blood of all living things.


The molecules that make up this substance consists of one hydrogen atom and two oxegen atoms. It is tasteless and odorless and can be in several different states such as its liquid form, ice as well as vapor. Vast bodies of water litter the earth and when the sun heats it up, the water evaporates and ascends into the sky in the form of clouds. When these clouds become dense enough, or when other factors are met, they begin to fall as rain, littering the planet with their tiny yet numerous tears.


One potential danger can be from the lack thereof. Nearly all creatures need some amount of water to survive. If given enough time, dehydration can settle in leading to headaches, feeling thirsty, tiredness and potentially death. On the flip side, too much water can lead to bloating, nausea, vomiting and could also lead to death. Making sure to ration the required amount of water throughout the day is key to avoiding this situation. On average, adults need around 10 to 15 cups of water per day.


Since we all need it, filthy water can also prove hazardous for drink. If one ever finds themselves in the wilderness, it is best to check the bodies of water for any odor or colorations. Even then, without proper equipment for conversion, drinking from open pools can be lead dangerous. One tip for this situation is to look for any running water, preferably running down a sheer vertical incline. While not a garuntee, water that runs down giant rocks, and earthy walls are more likely to be drinkable as the act of falling against the surface helps clean it. From there, a bottle could be used to catch it and subsequently boiling the liquid can help purify the content.
